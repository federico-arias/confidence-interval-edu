Parameter estimation

All alone in the night, left alone to ponder about the ultimate meaning of life, we all are confronted with the important questions: is Harry Styles really the sexiest One Direction member?, do 17 year old ladies prefer Prince Harry or the more daunting Prince Henry? The posibility of finding an answer seems far away. How are we ever going to find a definite answer to these vexing questions if we are unable to survey the opinion of every 17 year old girl about the princes or of every directioner? (you cannot hastly reach to such a conclusion without empirically backed research, after all).

Well, fear not. There is a way to find some characteristic from a big population without surveying it in its entirety. What's that wizardy, you ask? Let me show you.

Suppose for a moment that you wanted to find the mean height of 500 people, but you were too lazy to ask their height to each and every one of them. What do you do? You only ask a couple of them. Let's say you ask 30 of them. This 30 people constitute a sample, that is, a portion of the total population from which you are estimating a parameter (in this case, the mean height).

If we take 20 samples of size 30 from our population of 500, our sample mean is going to be, pretty close to 170, the population mean.

Click on the samples to know the mean of every sample.

Confidence interval

As we saw, if we took a sample of small size, we can get a fairly close estimation of the parameter. However, this is not always the case. Sometimes, due to chance, our sample might not be so close to our population parameter. Take, for instance, sample number 4, or sample number 10, which are far away from the population parameter.

But, we can observe that, from all the patterns that we took from the population, most of them are near and very few of them are away.

From our samples, we can say that30 of them are between 120 and 160.

Normally distributed samples
